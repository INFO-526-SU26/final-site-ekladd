Background

Why Study Gas Price Volatility?

  • Gas prices surged above $4/gallon nationwide in spring 2026, the highest levels since 2022.1

  • Geopolitical events and supply disruptions (particularly conflict affecting Middle East oil supplies and the Strait of Hormuz) triggered rapid week-to-week swings in crude oil and gasoline prices.

  • This raises an interesting question: How unusual are large gasoline price spikes, and how has price volatility changed over the past 35 years?

  1. Source: Gas prices hit $4 a gallon; Kuwaiti tanker struck off Dubai - The Washington Post

Historical Dataset

  • The dataset is from the TidyTuesday github repo from 2025-07-01. It looks at the weekly US gas prices for both regular and diesel. The date range of this data set is from Aug 1990 to June 2025.

  • “The data comes from the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA), which publishes average retail gasoline and diesel prices each Monday.”1

  1. Source: Gasoline and Diesel Fuel Update - U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA)

Conclusions

  1. Question 1: Are sudden spikes in gas prices common?

    • Conclusion: No, no change from week to week is the most common, with only a small percentage falling in the “spike” category.

  2. Question 2: Does stock market uncertainty coincide with increased gas prices?

    • Conclusion: There are some instances when volatility in gas prices coincides with volatility in the stock market. But there are other instances where they behave opposite of one another.

  3. Bonus Question 3: Are gas price spikes associated with major geopolitical or supply-disruption events?

    • Conclusion: More analysis and data collection would be needed for a more definitive conclusion, but for a rough estimate shows that top 1% spikes occured within an 8 week window of Hurricane Katrina, the 2008 oil price shock and Russia invading Ukraine.
